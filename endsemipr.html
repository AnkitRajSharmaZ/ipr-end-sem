Part 1: IPR & Social Development

(Source: WIPO Document)

1. Defining Social Development

It's broad, but for this exam, it means two things:

  • Establishment of markets and institutions that help living standards grow sustainably.
  • Increasing the ability of society (govt, schools, hospitals) to meet citizens' needs for well-being.

KEY CONCEPT: IPR is not enough on its own. It must be embedded in a broader system including:

  • Innovation & Growth policies.
  • Competition & Regulation.
  • Education, Public Health, & Access to Knowledge.
The "Promised Gains" of Global IPR

Advocates say a strong system brings these 8 benefits:

  • Innovation Incentives: Motivates people to invent worldwide.
  • Help for Poor: Encourages R&D for products poor countries need.
  • Trade & Investment: Expands trade in high-tech goods.
  • Knowledge Transfer: Creates a market to sell/share ideas across borders.
  • Consumer Protection: Guarantees where a product comes from (Origin).
  • Creativity: Builds markets for creative works (music, art).
  • Price Differentiation: Allows charging different prices in different markets.
  • Traditional Knowledge: Protects local/indigenous wisdom.
The "Potential Costs" (Downsides)
  • High Admin Costs: Enforcement and running the system is expensive.
  • Market Power: Can lead to monopolies if competition rules are weak.
  • Licensing Abuse: Companies might refuse to license or charge too much.
  • Blocking Innovation: Unbalanced rules can stop others from improving an invention.
  • Higher Prices: Medicines, farming seeds, and green tech might get expensive.
  • Restricted Access: Strict copyright makes it hard to get educational/scientific materials.

2. The Evidence from Research

(Warning: Research is hard because data is scarce and causation is tricky!)

A. Impact on Innovation

  • Rich Countries: No big increase in innovation just because of IPR.
  • Emerging Economies (China/India): Yes, they are innovating and exporting more tech.
  • Poor Countries: Little evidence that IPR helps them innovate.
  • Trademarks: Helpful for building product markets in developing nations.

B. Technology Transfer (Sharing Tech)

  • Stronger IPR helps bring tech into a country through market channels (selling/licensing).
  • The "Threshold" Effect: This only works if the country has:
    • Educated people (Human Capital).
    • Effective domestic competition.
    • Good governance & infrastructure.
  • It does NOT work well for the poorest countries.

C. Pharmaceuticals (Drugs) & R&D

  • Rich Countries: Stronger laws = More R&D.
  • Developing Countries: Stronger laws = NO increase in local R&D.
  • Neglected Diseases: No increase in private research for diseases affecting the poor.
  • India Case Study: After 2005 patent law, big Indian firms spent more on R&D, but mostly for exports, not for local needs.

D. Drug Prices & Availability

  • Fear: Prices would skyrocket (100-300%).
  • Reality: Increases were modest (3% to 20%) likely due to price regulations.
  • Availability: Patents can delay new drug launches.
  • Benefit: Reduced dangerous counterfeit (fake) medicines.

E. Access to Knowledge

  • Patents do not pay for "adaptation" (changing tech to fit local needs).
  • Rigorous Copyright raises the cost of textbooks and learning materials.

3. Policy Lessons for Developing Countries

How should they handle IPR?

  • Transparency: Make the system clear and predictable.
  • Use "Exceptions" & Loopholes:
    • Patents: Set high standards (don't patent weak inventions), use "Compulsory Licensing" (govt forces license in emergency), Research exemptions.
    • Trade Secrets: Keep definitions narrow.
    • Copyrights: Allow "Fair Use" for education.
  • Broader Strategy (Crucial):
    • Improve investment climate.
    • Build local R&D capacity in firms.
    • Invest in Science & Tech Education.
    • Subsidize research into local adaptation.
    • Regulate prices (especially medicines).

Global Policy Lessons

  • Create "Knowledge Pools": Publicly funded science available for cheap licensing.
  • Mobility Visas: Let skilled workers move around easily.

Part 2: International Business (IB)

(Source: Pearson Textbook)

1. Key Definitions

  • International Business: Trade and investment activities by firms across national borders.
  • Globalization of Markets: Economies becoming integrated and dependent on each other.
  • International Trade: Exchange of products (goods) AND services.
  • Exporting: Selling abroad (e.g., Boeing sells planes).
  • Importing (Global Sourcing): Buying from abroad (e.g., Toyota buys parts from China).
Important Distinction: Investment Types
  • International Investment (FDI): Transfer of assets to another country (e.g., building a factory). *Focus of this course.*
  • International Portfolio Investment: Passive ownership (buying stocks/bonds) just for financial returns.

2. The "Flows" of IB

It's not just products! It includes flows of:

  • Knowledge
  • Trade in Products
  • Trade in Services
  • Direct Investments

3. Service Industries are Going Global

Many service sectors are rapidly internationalizing:

  • Architectural/Construction: building dams, airports (e.g., ABB, Bechtel).
  • Banking/Insurance: (e.g., HSBC, Bank of America).
  • Education: Training & publishing (e.g., Pearson, Berlitz).
  • Entertainment: Movies, music (e.g., Sony, Time Warner).
  • Professional Services: Legal, Accounting (e.g., McKinsey, Ernst & Young).

4. The Four Risks of IB (Memorize This!)

Firms face 4 major risks when going abroad:

1. Cross-Cultural Risk

  • Cultural Differences: Language, religion, customs.
  • Negotiation Patterns: Mexicans are friendly/social first; Americans are assertive/fast.
  • Decision-Making: Japanese take time; Canadians are decisive ("shoot from the hip").
  • Ethics: Bribery might be accepted in some places, illegal in others.
  • Ethical Example: Sandblasting Jeans. Factory workers die from lung disease (silicosis) to make "faded" jeans. Illegal in EU/US, but still done in poor countries.

2. Country Risk (Political)

  • Govt Intervention: Barriers to trade, protectionism.
  • Bureaucracy: Red tape, corruption (bribes required in Russia).
  • IPR Issues: Weak legal systems failing to protect ideas.
  • Instability: War, economic mismanagement.
  • Examples: Venezuela interfering with foreign firms; Argentina's high inflation.

3. Currency Risk (Financial)

  • Currency Exposure: Exchange rates change (Japanese Yen fluctuates).
  • Asset Valuation: Value of your factory drops if local currency drops.
  • Taxation: Income/sales taxes vary (US has high corporate tax).
  • Inflation: Makes pricing hard (Brazil/Turkey have high inflation).

4. Commercial Risk

  • Weak Partners: Your local partner fails.
  • Operational Problems: Poor execution of strategy.
  • Timing: Entering the market too early or too late.
  • Competition: Intense rivals.

5. Who Participates?

  • MNE (Multinational Enterprise): Large, wealthy, subsidiaries worldwide (Disney, Samsung, Caterpillar, Vodafone).
  • SME (Small/Medium Enterprise): < 500 employees. Make up 90% of all firms. Increasingly active globally.
  • Born Global Firm: Young, entrepreneurial startup that does IB right from the start.
  • NGOs (Non-Governmental): Non-profits.
    • Examples: Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation (Health/Education), CARE (Poverty), Wellcome Trust.

6. Why Do Firms Internationalize?

  • Growth: Diversify markets (Harley-Davidson, IKEA, H&M).
  • Margins/Profit: Foreign markets are often more profitable.
  • New Ideas: GM learned to make small fuel-efficient cars in Europe.
  • Follow Customers: Toyota suppliers went to Britain when Toyota opened a factory there.
  • Sourcing: Access cheap/better factors of production (Apple sources worldwide, Sony manufactures in China).
  • Economies of Scale: Bigger volume = lower cost per unit (Airbus).
  • Confront Competitors: Haier (China) came to the US to learn about and compete with Whirlpool.
  • Relationships: French firm Groupe Bull partnered with Toshiba.

7. Why Study IB?

  • Facilitator: Connects the global economy.
  • Economic Well-being: Fuels growth and living standards.
  • Competitive Advantage: Good for the firm, good for YOUR career.
  • Global Corporate Citizenship: Firms must be ethical (IB affects everyone).

Case Study: Facebook

  • Global Phenomenon: 1.3 Billion users (1 in 6 people).
  • Reach: 70% of users live outside the US.
  • Lesson: Shows how converging lifestyles & tech create global enterprises.

Case Study: Ashley Lumb

  • Majors: Marketing & IB.
  • Jobs: Nice (France), Monte Carlo, Rome (Italian Vogue).
  • Success Factors: Hard work, Networking (met lots of people), Researched markets, Worked "unglamorous" jobs to stay afloat.
  • Challenges: Stepping out of comfort zone, Language barriers.
